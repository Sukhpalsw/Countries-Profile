History

Sweden history dates back to prehistoric times, but it gained prominence during the Viking Age (8th 11th centuries). In the Middle Ages, it was part of the Kalmar Union with Denmark and Norway. Sweden became a major European power in the 17th century during the reign of King Gustavus Adolphus. It remained neutral during both World Wars, which helped preserve its infrastructure and economy. Sweden is known for its long-standing tradition of peace, democracy, and humanitarian values.

Culture

Swedish culture emphasizes equality, sustainability, design, and social welfare. The concept of “lagom” (meaning “just the right amount”) influences everything from work-life balance to food and fashion. Sweden is a hub for music, literature, and design—home to ABBA, IKEA, and Nobel laureates like Selma Lagerlöf. Celebrations like Midsummer, Lucia Day, and Walpurgis Night are culturally significant.

Religion

Sweden is a secular country, though historically Lutheran Christianity has been dominant. The Church of Sweden (Evangelical Lutheran) was the state church until 2000. Today, many Swedes identify as non-religious, but religious freedom is respected and other religions like Islam, Catholicism, Buddhism, and Judaism are practiced.

Population

Sweden has a population of about 10.6 million people (as of 2024). It is ethnically diverse due to immigration and is known for being welcoming to refugees. The capital, Stockholm, is the largest city, followed by Gothenburg and Malmö.

Income and GDP

Sweden has a high-income, mixed economy. The GDP is around $700 billion USD, with strong sectors in technology, manufacturing, services, and green energy. It ranks high in human development, innovation, and income equality, though like most nations, faces challenges like housing prices and demographic shifts.

Parliamentary System

    Head of states

  • The King of Sweden, currently King Carl XVI Gustaf, serves as the ceremonial head of state. The monarchy has no real political power, and the King's duties are limited to formal and symbolic roles such as opening the parliament and representing Sweden abroad.

    • The Riksdag (Parliament)

  • It consists of 349 members.

  • Members are elected through proportional representation for a four-year term.

  • The Riksdag passes laws, approves the national budget, and monitors the government.

    • Elections

  • General elections are held every 4 years.
  • All Swedish citizens aged 18 and over can vote.

    • Political Parties


  • Multi-party democracy: Sweden has several active political parties, including the Social Democrats, Moderates, Sweden Democrats, Centre Party, and others.
